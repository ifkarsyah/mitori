Here's the practical core — the stuff that shows up in nearly every conversation, versus the long tail of patterns that are mostly for reading/writing or exam nuance.

## 1. Particles are the skeleton
Japanese word order is flexible because particles mark grammatical role, not position. Six carry almost all daily conversation:
- **は** — topic ("as for X...")
- **が** — subject/emphasis (subtly different from は — the classic hard-to-explain pair, but daily use is learnable by pattern before theory)
- **を** — direct object
- **に / で** — に for time/destination/indirect object, で for location-of-action/means
- **の** — possession/linking nouns
- **と / も** — "and/with" for people, "also"

## 2. The て-form is the hub of the language
This is the single highest-leverage thing to master. Once you can form it, it unlocks most of daily grammar by just attaching things to it:
- 食べて + ください → please eat
- 食べて + いる → eating (progressive/state)
- 食べて + もいい → allowed to eat
- 食べて + はいけない → not allowed to eat
- 食べて + みる → try eating
- 食べて + おく → eat in preparation
- 食べて、飲んで、寝た → sequencing actions

If you only drill one conjugation hard, make it this one.

## 3. Register: plain form vs です/ます
Daily life constantly switches between polite (strangers, coworkers, service) and plain/casual (friends, family, inner thoughts). You need both forms of everything — this isn't optional politeness, it's a structural axis of the grammar (plain form is also what's used inside longer sentences, e.g. before と思う).

## 4. A small set of workhorse patterns
These cover a disproportionate share of real speech:
- **〜たい** — want to do
- **〜たら** — if/when (the most flexible, casual conditional — skip ば/と/なら until たら feels natural)
- **〜と思う** — I think
- **〜んです** — explanatory nuance ("the thing is...") — extremely common in natural speech, rarely taught early
- **あげる/くれる/もらう** — giving and receiving, direction-of-favor matters a lot socially in Japanese
- **〜ことができる / 動詞可能形** — ability

## 5. Sentence-final particles
ね (seeking agreement), よ (asserting new info), か (question), わ (softening) — these get skipped in formal grammar study but carry enormous conversational nuance. Native daily speech barely exists without them.

---

Everything else — causative-passive, formal written forms, most of the "hundred patterns" — is long-tail: useful eventually, low-frequency in daily talk.
