# ちょっと - "a little" / softening hedge

Literally "a little/a bit," but its real daily job is softening requests, refusals, and criticism — one of the most reflexively used words in polite/casual Japanese to avoid sounding blunt.

## Literal "a little"
The base meaning, quantifying something as small in degree or amount:

- ちょっと待ってください。— Please wait a moment.
- ちょっと高いですね。— [It]'s a bit expensive, isn't it.
- ちょっとだけ食べた。— [I] ate just a little. (see [[9a-だけ]] in 1-particle for the stacking with だけ)

## Softening a refusal ("that's a bit...")
ちょっと is the go-to way to decline or express reluctance without directly saying no — trailing off is completely normal and understood as a soft "no":

- 今日はちょっと... — Today's a bit... [not good for me]. (trails off, understood as declining)
- それはちょっと難しいです。— That's a bit difficult. (softer than 無理です/できません — "impossible/can't do it")

Leaving the sentence unfinished after ちょっと is not rude or incomplete — it's the expected, polite way to say no in many daily situations, especially declining invitations. See [[../8-conjunction/2-trailing-off]] for this same trailing-off habit across けど/し/から/て-form.

## Softening a request or interruption
Similar to あの (see [[2-あの]]), ちょっと often opens a request to make it feel smaller/less imposing:

- ちょっと聞いてもいいですか。— Can I ask [you] something [real quick]?
- ちょっとすみません。— Excuse me a moment. (getting past someone, interrupting briefly)

## Softening criticism or a negative observation
Cushions a critical or negative statement so it lands less harshly:

- それはちょっと... — That's a bit [off/not great]. (implied criticism, unfinished)
- ちょっとうるさいよ。— [That]'s a little loud, y'know. (softer than 直接 うるさい! alone)

## Notes
- ちょっと is safe across both polite and casual registers — one of the most universally usable softeners in the language, unlike casual-only fillers such as なんか (see [[1-なんか]] in 5-filler).
- ちょっと + trailing silence (no verb after) is a distinct, very common pattern worth specifically getting comfortable with — it's not a sentence fragment mistake, it's how native speakers decline things gracefully.
