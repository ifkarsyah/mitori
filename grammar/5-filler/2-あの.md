# あの / あのー - hesitation / attention-getter

Opens a sentence to get someone's attention, hesitate before saying something (often awkward or a request), or politely interrupt. One of the most reflexive fillers in Japanese — often the very first sound out of someone's mouth when approaching a stranger.

## Getting someone's attention / approaching a stranger
The standard, polite way to open an interaction with someone you don't know — a waiter, a stranger on the street, a store clerk:

- あの、すみません。— Um, excuse me.
- あのー、これはいくらですか。— Um, how much is this?

Functionally similar to "excuse me" or "um" in English as a conversation-opener, not a request itself — it just signals "I'm about to say something to you."

## Hesitation before a request or awkward topic
あの (often lengthened to あのー while thinking) buys a beat before saying something potentially uncomfortable, asking a favor, or bringing up a sensitive topic:

- あの、ちょっとお願いがあるんですが。— Um, [I] have a bit of a favor to ask...
- あのー、実は... — Um, actually...

Pairs naturally with the softening んですが/んですけど pattern (see [[1-んです]] in 4-pattern and [[12-けど]] in 1-particle).

## Length signals hesitation level
A short あの is a quick attention-getter; a drawn-out あのー signals more hesitation or that the speaker is actively formulating what to say next — similar to how "um..." can be short or stretched out in English depending on how much someone is stalling.

## Notes
- あの is polite/neutral register — safe with strangers, service staff, and in formal contexts, unlike more casual fillers like なんか (see [[1-なんか]]).
- Don't confuse this あの with あの as a demonstrative adjective ("that ~ over there," as in あの人 — that person) — same word, different job; context (start of a sentence with nothing following directly = filler; あの + noun = demonstrative) makes it clear.
