# やっぱり / やっぱ - "as I thought" / "after all"

Signals that a conclusion matches what you suspected, or marks a change of mind back to an earlier instinct. One of the most frequently used words in casual Japanese conversation — やっぱ is the even more casual, clipped version.

## Confirming a hunch ("as I thought" / "I knew it")
Used when reality confirms what you already suspected or predicted:

- やっぱり雨が降った。— [It] rained after all, [just as I thought].
- やっぱり彼は来なかった。— [As expected], he didn't come.
- やっぱりそうだと思った! — I knew it! (lit. "I thought it was as I expected, after all")

## Reverting to an earlier choice ("after all," "on second thought")
Marks changing your mind back toward something you'd considered before, or reconsidering a decision mid-stream:

- やっぱりコーヒーにする。— [I]'ll have coffee after all. (changed mind, back to original instinct)
- やっぱりやめよう。— Let's not, after all. / On second thought, let's not.
- 行くか迷ったけど、やっぱり行くことにした。— [I] was torn about going, but decided to go after all.

This "changing your mind, landing back on your gut instinct" use is extremely common in daily decision-making speech — ordering food, making plans, etc.

## Standalone reaction
やっぱり! by itself is a common one-word reaction meaning "I knew it!" / "just as expected" — reacting to news that confirms a suspicion.

## やっぱ — casual clip
やっぱ is just やっぱり with the last syllable dropped, used identically but sounds more casual/quick — common in relaxed spoken Japanese:

- やっぱ無理。— Nah, can't do it after all.

## Notes
- やっぱり doesn't attach grammatically to anything — it's a standalone adverb that sits at the start of (or floats within) a sentence, unlike particles that attach directly to words.
- Distinct from もう一度 (once more) or また (again) — やっぱり is specifically about matching an expectation or reverting to an earlier inclination, not repetition.
