# なんか - filler / "like" / "something"

One of the most common words in casual spoken Japanese, doing three related jobs: a thinking filler, a casual "something," and a non-exhaustive example marker. If you listen to any natural conversation, なんか shows up constantly.

## Pure filler ("like," "um," "sort of")
Dropped in at the start of a sentence or mid-thought while organizing what to say, or to soften a statement — functionally close to English "like" as a filler:

- なんか、疲れた。— Like, [I'm] tired. / Somehow [I'm] tired.
- なんか、今日変な日だった。— Today was, like, a weird day.
- それ、なんか違うと思う。— I think that's, like, not quite right.

This use carries no fixed literal meaning — it's a hedge/pause word, similar to how "like" or "kind of" gets inserted in casual English speech. Overusing it is a real tic (native speakers do it too, in bursts), but as a learner it's more important to *recognize* it than to force it into your own speech constantly.

## Casual contraction of 何か ("something")
なんか is the everyday spoken contraction of 何か (see the indefinite-pronoun note in [[7-か]]):

- なんか食べたい。— [I] want to eat something.
- なんか変な音がした。— [I] heard some kind of weird sound.
- なんか用? — [You] need something [from me]? / What's up? (casual, slightly blunt)

Compare with the filler use: 何か食べたい (want to eat *something*, specific unspecified object) vs なんか、食べたい (like, [I] want to eat — filler softening the whole sentence, no specific "something" implied). Context and what follows なんか usually disambiguates.

## Non-exhaustive example marker ("things like X")
Attaches after a noun to suggest it as one example among similar things — overlaps with とか (see [[13-とか]]) but slightly more casual/dismissive in tone, sometimes implying mild distance or a lack of enthusiasm:

- コーヒーなんか好きです。— [I] like things like coffee. (coffee as one example of what I like)
- 一人でなんか行けないよ。— [I] can't go alone or anything [like that]. (なんか reinforcing "there's no way")

## Vague dissatisfaction / "something's off" feeling
なんか often precedes a vague, hard-to-pin-down feeling or impression — "somehow," "for some reason":

- なんか変。— Something's weird [about this]. (can't quite say what)
- なんか嫌だ。— [I] just don't like it, somehow. (no clear reason given)

## Notes
- なんか is almost exclusively spoken/casual — avoid it in polite or written contexts; そうですね-style fillers or a clean sentence without it are better there.
- Don't confuse なんか (filler/something/example-marker, this file) with なにか, its full, unabbreviated written form — they're the same word, なんか is just the natural spoken contraction.
