# Counters (助数詞) — how to count things

Japanese doesn't count "two books" the way English does — every noun category has its own counter suffix attached to a number, and the numbers themselves sometimes shift sound depending on which counter follows. Survival-critical: ordering food, buying things, giving your age, telling time, counting people, all route through this system.

## Two number systems
Japanese has native Japanese numbers (used for a small, closed set of generic counting) and Sino-Japanese numbers (used with virtually all counters):

**Native (native Japanese, only goes 1–10, then switches to Sino-Japanese):**
ひとつ、ふたつ、みっつ、よっつ、いつつ、むっつ、ななつ、やっつ、ここのつ、とお

Used generically for objects with no specific counter, or when you don't know/remember the right counter — a safe fallback: りんごを 3つ ください。— [Give me] 3 apples, please. (using つ instead of 個)

**Sino-Japanese (the base system nearly all counters attach to):**
いち、に、さん、よん(し)、ご、ろく、なな(しち)、はち、きゅう(く)、じゅう

4, 7, and 9 each have two readings (よん/し, なな/しち, きゅう/く) — which one is used depends on the specific counter or context; both exist because し and しち sound too similar and get avoided in speech, and し/く carry unlucky death/suffering associations avoided in certain contexts (hospitals, gift quantities).

## Common counters

| Counter | Used for | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 人 (にん/り) | people | 一人 (ひとり), 二人 (ふたり), 三人 (さんにん) — irregular for 1 and 2! |
| つ | generic objects (native numbers) | 一つ、二つ、三つ |
| 個 (こ) | small general objects | 一個、二個、三個 |
| 枚 (まい) | flat things (paper, tickets, plates) | 一枚、二枚 |
| 本 (ほん/ぼん/ぽん) | long cylindrical things (bottles, pens, trains) | 一本 (いっぽん), 二本 (にほん), 三本 (さんぼん) |
| 冊 (さつ) | bound things (books, magazines) | 一冊、二冊 |
| 匹 (ひき/びき/ぴき) | small animals (cats, dogs, bugs) | 一匹 (いっぴき), 二匹 (にひき) |
| 頭 (とう) | large animals (cows, elephants) | 一頭、二頭 |
| 台 (だい) | machines, vehicles | 一台、二台 |
| 階 (かい/がい) | floors of a building | 一階 (いっかい), 二階 (にかい) |
| 歳/才 (さい) | age | 一歳、二十歳 (はたち — irregular!) |
| 円 (えん) | yen | 百円、千円 |
| 時 (じ) | hour (clock time) | 一時、二時、四時 (よじ, not よんじ) |
| 分 (ふん/ぷん) | minutes | 一分 (いっぷん), 二分 (にふん), 三分 (さんぷん) |

## The sound-shift trap
Certain counters starting with は/ひ/ふ (本、匹、分, etc.) trigger consonant shifts on 1, 3, 6, 8, and 10 — this is genuinely irregular and just needs memorizing per counter, following patterns like:

- 一 + 本/匹/分/階 → いっぽん/いっぴき/いっぷん/いっかい (っ + p-sound)
- 三 + 本 → さんぼん (b-sound, not h)
- 六/八/十 + 本 → ろっぽん/はっぽん/じゅっぽん (っ + p-sound again)

There's no universal shortcut — but the pattern of "1, 3, 6, 8, 10 are the troublemakers with は-row counters" is worth knowing so you're not caught off guard.

## 人 — the irregular counter for people
The most commonly used counter has genuinely irregular readings for 1 and 2, then becomes regular:

- 一人 (ひとり) — one person
- 二人 (ふたり) — two people
- 三人 (さんにん), 四人 (よにん), 五人 (ごにん)... — regular にん from 3 onward

## Asking "how many?"
The question word matches the counting system in use:

- いくつ — how many (with native つ counting, or asking age casually: おいくつですか — how old are you, polite)
- 何 + counter — how many, with Sino-Japanese counters: 何人 (なんにん, how many people), 何個 (なんこ, how many pieces), 何歳 (なんさい, how old)

## Notes
- When unsure of the exact counter for something, つ (native numbers) is a safe generic fallback for physical objects — natives will understand even if it's not the "textbook precise" counter.
- The most natural position for a number+counter is right after the noun's particle, floating before the verb: りんごを3つ買った。— [I] bought 3 apples. Placing it before the noun with の (3つのりんごを買った) is also grammatical and common, just a slightly different emphasis — both are fine to use.
