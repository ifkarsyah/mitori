# あげる / くれる / もらう — giving and receiving

Three verbs for "give/receive," but the choice isn't about who physically hands what to whom — it's about **direction relative to the speaker's in-group**, and getting it backwards is a common, noticeable learner mistake. Core per [[0-overview]].

## The three verbs

**あげる** — I/someone give to someone else (away from the speaker's perspective)
**くれる** — someone gives to me/my in-group (toward the speaker's perspective)
**もらう** — I/someone receive from someone else (receiving, direction-neutral on the giving side)

The key mental model: あげる and くれる both mean "give," but あげる is used when the giving moves *away* from you (you→someone, or A→B where you're not involved), while くれる is used when the giving moves *toward* you (someone→you). もらう flips the frame entirely — it's from the receiver's viewpoint regardless of direction.

## あげる — giving away from yourself
- 私は友達にプレゼントをあげました。— I gave my friend a present.
- 田中さんは山田さんに本をあげました。— Tanaka gave Yamada a book. (neither party is "me," but the giving is described from the giver's side, away from them)

Using あげる for something given *to you* sounds off — 友達は私にプレゼントをあげました is grammatically parseable but unnatural; native speakers reach for くれる instead.

## くれる — giving toward yourself (or your in-group)
- 友達が私にプレゼントをくれました。— My friend gave me a present.
- 田中さんが弟にお金をくれました。— Tanaka gave my little brother money. (in-group extends to family/close circle, not just literal "me")

The giver takes が (not は as topic, typically — が marks who's doing the giving, since the interesting/new info is often who gave it):

- 誰がくれたの? — Who gave [it to you]?

## もらう — receiving (viewpoint flip)
Structured as [receiver]は[giver]に/から[thing]をもらう — receiver is the topic/subject, giver marked with に or から:

- 私は友達にプレゼントをもらいました。— I received a present from my friend. (= my friend gave me a present, same event as the くれる example above, opposite grammatical framing)
- 誕生日に何をもらいましたか。— What did [you] get for your birthday?

あげる/くれる describe the event from the giver's side; もらう describes the same event from the receiver's side — often either works for the same real-world event, and the choice is about whose perspective you want to center.

## Quick decision table

| Direction | Verb | Giver marked with | Topic/subject |
|---|---|---|---|
| Me → someone else | あげる | は/が (giver = topic) | giver |
| Someone → me | くれる | が (giver) | giver, but centers on me as recipient |
| I receive from someone | もらう | に/から (giver) | receiver (me) is topic |

## て-form attachment: favors, not just objects
These three attach to て-form to describe doing an action *as a favor*, not just handing over a thing — this is extremely common in daily speech (see [[2-て-form]]):

- 手伝ってあげる。— I'll help [you] [as a favor, from me outward].
- 手伝ってくれる? — Will [you] help me [as a favor toward me]? (a very common, natural way to ask for help)
- 先生に教えてもらいました。— [I] had the teacher explain [it] [to me]. / The teacher explained it for me.

てくれる and てもらう both describe someone doing something that benefits you, but with different subjects: 教えてくれる (the teacher is the subject, doing it toward me) vs 教えてもらう (I'm the subject, receiving the favor).

## Notes
- あげる toward a social superior can sound presumptuous/condescending (implies you're bestowing something) — さしあげる is the humble alternative in formal contexts; not needed for casual daily use, just good to recognize.
- くれる is the one learners most often get backwards early on, since English "give" doesn't grammaticalize this directional split — if in doubt, ask "did this benefit *me*?" → くれる/もらう territory, not あげる.
