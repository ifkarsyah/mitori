# Plain vs. polite (です/ます) form

Japanese runs on two parallel registers for nearly every verb and adjective. This isn't optional politeness dressing — see [[0-overview]] point 3 — it's a structural axis: certain grammar patterns require plain form even inside an otherwise polite sentence.

## The two registers

**Polite (です/ます)** — used with strangers, coworkers, service interactions, formal writing, and by default when unsure:

- 食べます / 食べません / 食べました / 食べませんでした
- 学生です / 学生ではありません (or じゃないです, more casual-polite)

**Plain (辞書形)** — used with friends, family, inner thought, casual writing, and *inside* longer sentences regardless of the overall politeness level:

- 食べる / 食べない / 食べた / 食べなかった
- 学生だ / 学生じゃない

## Conjugation table (verbs)

| | Polite | Plain |
|---|---|---|
| Non-past affirmative | 食べます | 食べる |
| Non-past negative | 食べません | 食べない |
| Past affirmative | 食べました | 食べた |
| Past negative | 食べませんでした | 食べなかった |

う-verb plain negative: drop -u, add -anai (行く→行かない, 話す→話さない — う irregularly becomes わ: 買う→買わない). Plain past follows the same sound-shift as て-form (see [[2-て-form]]): 行って→行った.

## Copula (だ/です) table

| | Polite | Plain |
|---|---|---|
| Affirmative | 学生です | 学生だ |
| Negative | 学生じゃないです | 学生じゃない |
| Past affirmative | 学生でした | 学生だった |
| Past negative | 学生じゃなかったです | 学生じゃなかった |

だ is often dropped entirely in casual speech when it's the final word of a plain sentence (学生。just standing alone) — but it reappears before だろう, だと思う, etc.

## Why plain form is required mid-sentence
A long list of grammar patterns attach to plain form regardless of the sentence's overall politeness — this is the "structural axis" point:

- 〜と思う: 雨が降ると思います。— I think it'll rain. (降る is plain, even though the sentence ends politely with 思います)
- 〜んです: 疲れたんです。— [explaining:] I'm tired. (plain 疲れた + んです, not 疲れましたんです)
- Before ようだ, らしい, し, けど (casual), and inside relative clauses modifying nouns: 昨日買った本 — the book [I] bought yesterday (買った is plain even in an otherwise polite conversation)

Mixing this up — trying ます before と思う, for instance — is one of the most common beginner errors once sentences get longer than a single clause.

## When to use which
- Default to polite (です/ます) in speech with anyone you're not close to, and in first encounters — it's the safe default.
- Plain form is not "ruder," it's a different social register — used with close friends/family, in diaries/casual writing, in your own internal monologue (と思う clauses), and it's also what dictionaries, grammar explanations, and most example sentences default to as the "base" form.
- Within one conversation, don't randomly alternate です/ます and plain endings on main clauses — pick a register and stay consistent; only the mid-sentence plain-form rule above is an exception.

## Notes
- Plain non-past = dictionary form — the same form used for classifying conjugation groups in [[1-conjugation-group]].
- な-adjectives and the copula behave alike (だ/です pattern); い-adjectives conjugate on their own without needing だ/です for plain form (see the upcoming adjective notes) — い-adjective plain non-past is just the adjective itself: 高い, not 高いだ.
