# Volitional form — let's / I intend to

Expresses "let's [do]" (inviting someone) or "I will/intend to [do]" (stating your own resolve). Has both polite and plain registers, mirroring [[3-plain-vs-polite-form]].

## Polite volitional — ましょう
ます-stem (see [[1-conjugation-group]]) + ましょう:

- 食べる → 食べましょう — let's eat
- 行く → 行きましょう — let's go
- する → しましょう — let's do [it]

ましょうか softens it into a suggestion/offer rather than a direct proposal:

- 手伝いましょうか。— Shall [I] help?
- 一緒に行きましょうか。— Shall we go together?

## Plain volitional — group-dependent sound shift
**う-verbs**: change final -u to -o, add う:
- 行く → 行こう — let's go / I'll go
- 話す → 話そう — let's talk
- 買う → 買おう — let's buy

**る-verbs**: drop る, add よう:
- 食べる → 食べよう — let's eat
- 見る → 見よう — let's watch

**Irregulars**: する → しよう, 来る (kuru) → 来よう (こよう)

## Two readings: "let's" vs "I will"
Context (especially the presence of a listener to invite) decides which reading applies — the form itself doesn't distinguish:

- さあ、食べよう! — Alright, let's eat! (inviting)
- 明日から頑張ろうと思う。— I think I'll work hard starting tomorrow. (self-directed resolve, often paired with と思う — see [[5a-と]])

The self-directed "I will" reading pairs constantly with 〜と思う to soften a bare declaration of intent into "I'm thinking I'll...":

- 留学しようと思っています。— I'm thinking of studying abroad.

## Volitional vs たい vs plain future
Don't confuse these three, which all touch "future/intent" territory:

- 食べたい — want to eat (desire, not commitment)
- 食べよう — let's eat / I will eat (invitation or resolve)
- 食べる — will eat (plain non-past also covers simple future, no special "intent" nuance — 明日食べる just states a fact about tomorrow)

## Notes
- Volitional form + とする means "try to (about to)": 帰ろうとしたら、雨が降ってきた。— Just as [I] was about to head home, it started raining.
- ましょう said to someone senior/a stranger out of the blue can sound presumptuous (it implies you're both doing it together) — 〜ませんか (won't you...?) is often a politer way to propose the same thing: 一緒に行きませんか。— Won't [you] go with [me]?
