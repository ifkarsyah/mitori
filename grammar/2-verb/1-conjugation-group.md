# Verb conjugation groups

Japanese verbs split into three groups by how they conjugate. Everything downstream — て-form, plain form, potential, negative, conditional — is a mechanical rule applied on top of this grouping. Learn to classify a verb correctly first; the rest is pattern application. See [[2-て-form]] and [[3-plain-vs-polite-form]], both built directly on this.

## The three groups

**Group 1 — う-verbs (godan / "u-verbs")**
Dictionary form ends in -u, and the final kana is any u-row kana other than る preceded by an i/e sound (see the る-verb trap below):

- 話す (hanasu) — to speak
- 読む (yomu) — to read
- 買う (kau) — to buy
- 待つ (matsu) — to wait
- 帰る (kaeru) — to return (looks like a る-verb, isn't — see below)

**Group 2 — る-verbs (ichidan / "ru-verbs")**
Dictionary form ends in -iru or -eru, where removing る leaves a valid stem directly usable across all conjugations:

- 食べる (taberu) — to eat
- 見る (miru) — to see
- 起きる (okiru) — to get up
- 寝る (neru) — to sleep

**Group 3 — irregular**
Only two true irregulars, but they're extremely high-frequency:

- する — to do
- 来る (kuru) — to come

## The る-verb trap
Not every verb ending in -iru/-eru is a る-verb. Some are secretly う-verbs that happen to end in る:

- 帰る (kaeru) — to return → う-verb (conjugates 帰ります, not 帰えます)
- 入る (hairu) — to enter → う-verb
- 走る (hashiru) — to run → う-verb
- 知る (shiru) — to know → う-verb

There's no 100% reliable shortcut — these have to be memorized as exceptions. When in doubt, check the ます-form: real る-verbs drop る cleanly (食べる→食べます), fake ones (う-verbs in disguise) change the vowel (帰る→帰ります, not 帰ます).

## ます-stem (masu-stem)
The single most useful form to extract from any verb — nearly every conjugation pattern builds off it:

| Group | Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| う-verb | change final -u to -i | 話す → 話し |
| る-verb | drop る | 食べる → 食べ |
| irregular | する→し, 来る→来(き) | する → し, 来る → 来 |

Add ます for polite non-past: 話します, 食べます, します, 来ます (きます).

## Why this matters before anything else
て-form, た-form (plain past), potential form, passive, causative — all of them are group-dependent rules. If you can't reliably say "this is a う-verb" vs "this is a る-verb," every subsequent pattern becomes rote memorization per-verb instead of a rule applied once. Nail this first.

## Notes
- 一段 (ichidan) = "one grade" = る-verb (stem doesn't change). 五段 (godan) = "five grades" = う-verb (stem shifts across the five u-row vowels: か/き/く/け/こ etc.) — the Japanese grammar terms literally describe the mechanic.
- Dictionary form is also called plain non-past — see [[3-plain-vs-polite-form]].
