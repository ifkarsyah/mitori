# Potential form (可能形) — ability

Turns a verb into "can [verb]" — a single-word alternative to 〜ことができる (see [[0-overview]]'s workhorse list, which pairs the two). Group-dependent, built directly on [[1-conjugation-group]].

## Forming it

**う-verbs (Group 1)** — change the final -u to -e, add る:

- 読む → 読める — can read
- 話す → 話せる — can speak
- 行く → 行ける — can go
- 買う → 買える — can buy

**る-verbs (Group 2)** — drop る, add られる:

- 食べる → 食べられる — can eat
- 見る → 見られる — can see

**Irregulars**
- する → できる — can do
- 来る (kuru) → 来られる (こられる) — can come

The result is always a る-verb (Group 2) regardless of the original group, so it conjugates like 食べる from there: 食べられます, 食べられない, 食べられました, etc.

## The ら抜き言葉 shortcut (colloquial)
In casual speech, る-verb potentials commonly drop the ら: 食べられる → 食べれる, 見られる → 見れる. This is extremely common in spoken Japanese but still considered non-standard/incorrect in formal writing and on tests — recognize it, but default to the full られる form when producing polite or written Japanese.

## Object marking: を → が
Just like たい (see [[4-たい-form]]), potential form pulls the object toward が instead of を — this is the same "feeling/ability predicate" pull as 好き, ほしい, 分かる:

- 日本語を話す → 日本語が話せる。— [I] can speak Japanese.
- 漢字を読む → 漢字が読める。— [I] can read kanji.

を isn't strictly wrong here, but が is the more natural default.

## Potential vs 〜ことができる
Both mean "can do X," but potential form is shorter and more common in speech; ことができる is more common in writing, explanations, or when the verb's potential form would be awkward/ambiguous:

- 泳げます。/ 泳ぐことができます。— [I] can swim. (interchangeable)
- ことができる also works cleanly with する-verbs where できる already exists: 運転することができる vs the more natural 運転できる (する's own potential).

## Potential vs たい — don't confuse the two
たい = want to (desire); potential = can (ability). They stack: 食べられるものを食べたい。— [I] want to eat something [that I] am able to eat.

## Notes
- Potential form describes general capability/possibility, not a one-off achievement — for "I managed to do X [this one time]," other constructions (〜ことができた, context) are often more natural, but potential past (食べられた) is still fine and commonly used.
- Some verbs have a distinct, older potential form for う-verbs using れる (話される) which is actually the passive form — don't confuse conversationally derived 話せる (potential) with 話される (passive/honorific); they look similar but come from different mechanics.
