# たり〜たりする - listing representative actions

Lists a few example actions out of a larger, unstated set — "[I do] things like X and Y," not an exhaustive account of everything that happens. Built on the same sound-shift as plain past (た-form, see [[2-て-form]]), just with り attached.

## Forming it
Plain past (た-form) + り, repeated for each example action, ending in する (conjugated for tense/register):

- 休みの日は本を読んだり、映画を見たりします。— On days off, [I] do things like read books and watch movies.
- 週末は掃除したり、洗濯したりします。— On weekends, [I] do things like clean and do laundry.
- 公園で走ったり、散歩したりしました。— At the park, [I] did things like run and take walks.

The final する carries the actual tense/politeness — the individual たり verbs themselves don't need to match tense with each other or with する.

## Why not just use て-form or と for the list?
Compare to plain て-form sequencing (see [[2-て-form]]) or と's exhaustive "and" (see [[../1-particle/5a-と]]):

- 本を読んで、映画を見ました。— [I] read a book, [then] watched a movie. (て-form: a complete, sequential account of exactly what happened)
- 本を読んだり、映画を見たりしました。— [I] did things like read a book and watch a movie. (たり: examples from a longer, unstated list — implies other things happened too)

たり is the natural choice whenever you're giving a representative sample rather than a complete report — which covers a huge share of daily "what do you do on weekends" type conversation.

## Contradictory/alternating actions
たり also works well for describing back-and-forth or contradictory actions, emphasizing the alternation itself:

- 天気は良かったり悪かったりです。— The weather is sometimes good, sometimes bad.
- 行ったり来たりする。— [To] go back and forth. (a common fixed expression, lit. "going and coming")

## Single たり (less common, but valid)
たり can technically appear just once, still implying "among other things," though doubling it is far more natural and common in daily speech:

- テレビを見たりして、ゆっくりしました。— [I] relaxed, [doing things] like watching TV [among other things].

## Notes
- Don't confuse たり (listing representative/alternating actions) with たら (conditional, see [[6-conditional]]) — both come from the same た-stem sound-shift, but たり always needs する at the end and never means "if/when."
- Extremely common when answering casual "what do you usually do" questions — a natural, idiomatic way to avoid listing an exhaustive, overly precise account of your routine.
