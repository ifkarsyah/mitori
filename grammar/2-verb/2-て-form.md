# て-form

The single highest-leverage conjugation in Japanese — see [[0-overview]] point 2. Once you can produce it reliably, most of daily grammar is just attaching things to it. Built directly from the conjugation groups in [[1-conjugation-group]] — make sure that's solid first.

## Forming て-form by group

**る-verbs (Group 2) — trivial**
Drop る, add て:

- 食べる → 食べて
- 見る → 見て
- 起きる → 起きて

**Irregulars (Group 3)**
- する → して
- 来る (kuru) → 来て (きて)

**う-verbs (Group 1) — the sound-shift rules**
The ending determines the shift. These are worth drilling until automatic:

| Ending | Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| う, つ, る | → って | 買う→買って, 待つ→待って, 帰る→帰って |
| む, ぶ, ぬ | → んで | 読む→読んで, 遊ぶ→遊んで, 死ぬ→死んで |
| く | → いて | 書く→書いて |
| ぐ | → いで | 泳ぐ→泳いで |
| す | → して | 話す→話して |

Exception: 行く (iku, "to go") is irregular within this pattern — 行って, not 行いて.

Same shifts apply to plain past (た-form): って/んで/いて/いで/して → just swap the final て for た (行って→行った). Learning て-form well means た-form comes almost for free.

## What attaches to て-form
This is the part that makes て-form the "hub" — a small set of endings turn it into most of daily grammar:

- 食べて + ください → please eat
- 食べて + いる → eating / (state of having eaten, context-dependent)
- 食べて + もいい → allowed to eat
- 食べて + はいけない → not allowed to eat
- 食べて + みる → try eating
- 食べて + おく → eat in preparation / ahead of time
- 食べて + しまう → end up eating / eat completely (often regret/completion nuance)
- 食べて、飲んで、寝た → sequencing actions ("ate, drank, [then] slept")

## てくる / ていく — change over time
Two more て-form attachments, specifically for describing something unfolding across time rather than a fixed state. Both come from くる (to come) and いく (to go), but used abstractly rather than about physical movement:

**てくる — a change that's been happening up to now, moving toward the present**
- 天気が良くなってきた。— The weather's been getting better [and continues to now].
- 日本語が分かるようになってきた。— [I]'ve gradually come to understand Japanese.
- 雨が降ってきた。— It's started raining. (a change beginning, arriving at the present moment)

**ていく — a change that will continue on from now, moving toward the future**
- これからも頑張っていきます。— [I]'ll keep doing my best going forward.
- 少しずつ人口が減っていく。— The population will keep gradually decreasing [from here on].
- 子供はどんどん大きくなっていく。— Kids just keep growing bigger and bigger [as time goes on].

The core intuition: てくる pulls a trend up to *now* (often from the past), ていく pushes a trend forward from *now* (into the future). Both are common in daily narration about gradual change — weather, feelings, habits, life circumstances — not just literal coming/going.

## て-form for sequencing vs. simultaneous connecting
When chaining clauses with て, the actions are read as sequential (and often causally/temporally linked), not just "and":

- 顔を洗って、歯を磨いた。— [I] washed my face, [then] brushed my teeth.
- 熱があって、休みました。— [I] had a fever, so [I] rested. (て can carry a loose cause-effect reading)

## Notes
- て-form itself carries no tense — tense comes from whatever attaches after it, or from context if it ends a casual sentence (食べて! as a soft command).
- Get the う-verb sound-shift table (って/んで/いて/いで/して) to muscle memory before moving on — it's the one part of Japanese conjugation most worth over-drilling.
