# そう / みたい / らしい - appearance, seeming, hearsay

Three ways to say "looks like / seems / apparently," each with a distinct source of information: そう is a visual/sensory impression, みたい is a casual, general "seems like," and らしい is hearsay/what you heard secondhand. Mixing these up is a common source of subtle unnaturalness even at intermediate level.

## そう (様態) — visual/sensory impression, "looks like"
Attaches to the ます-stem of verbs (see [[1-conjugation-group]] in 2-verb) or the stem of い/な-adjectives (drop the final い or な), describing an impression formed by looking at something — before you've confirmed it:

- おいしそう。— [That] looks tasty. (haven't tasted it yet, judging by appearance)
- 雨が降りそう。— [It] looks like [it]'s about to rain. (based on the sky right now)
- 疲れてそう。— [You] look tired. (visual impression)
- 便利そう。— [That] looks convenient. (な-adjective stem, drop な: 便利(な)→便利そう)

Irregular: いい → よさそう (not いいそう), ない → なさそう.

This そう is about a *prediction/impression from what you currently perceive* — it's specifically not for something you've already confirmed as true (for that, just state it directly: おいしいです, not おいしいそうです in that sense — see hearsay そう below for the confusingly identical-looking but different construction).

## そう (伝聞) — hearsay, "I heard that" (different grammar, same word!)
A second, unrelated そう attaches to the **full plain form** (not the stem) and means "I heard that..." — reporting something you were told, not your own impression:

- 明日は雨が降るそうです。— I heard [it]'ll rain tomorrow. (from the news, someone else, etc.)
- 田中さんは忙しいそうです。— I heard Tanaka is busy.

The difference is entirely in what そう attaches to: stem (降りそう, appearance) vs. full plain form (降るそうです, hearsay) — easy to mix up, worth double-checking which one you mean.

## みたい — casual "seems like" (general-purpose, spoken)
The casual, catch-all version of both そう and らしい — attaches after the plain form of verbs/い-adjectives, or directly after nouns/な-adjective stems (no な needed before みたい):

- 雨みたい。— Seems like rain. (could be visual, could be secondhand — みたい doesn't specify the source)
- 元気みたい。— [They] seem energetic/well.
- 学生みたいです。— [They] seem to be a student.

みたい is the natural default in casual conversation when you don't need to be precise about *why* you think something — much more common in speech than carefully choosing between visual-そう and hearsay-そう.

## らしい — hearsay / "apparently" (also: "typical of")
Similar to hearsay-そう, but slightly more distanced/uncertain in tone, and also carries a secondary "typical of X" meaning. Attaches after plain form (with the same noun/な-adjective exception as みたい — no だ needed):

- 明日は雨らしい。— Apparently [it]'ll rain tomorrow. (secondhand, slightly more hedge than そう)
- 彼は忙しいらしいです。— [I hear] he's busy.
- 子供らしい。— [That]'s childlike / typical of a child. (the "typical of" sense — distinct usage, worth recognizing)

## Quick comparison

| Form | Attaches to | Source of info |
|---|---|---|
| Xそう (appearance) | verb/adjective stem | visual impression, before confirming |
| Xそうです (hearsay) | full plain form | reported, "I heard" |
| Xみたい | plain form / noun | casual, source unspecified — safest default |
| Xらしい | plain form / noun | hearsay, more distanced; also "typical of" |

## Notes
- In casual daily conversation, みたい does most of the work — don't stress over precisely choosing between visual-そう and らしい early on.
- All four can stack with んです/けど for softening (see [[1-んです]]): 雨が降りそうなんですけど。— It looks like it might rain, so...
