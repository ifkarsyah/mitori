# かな - "I wonder" (self-directed musing)

Marks a thought as uncertain, musing, or half-directed at yourself rather than a real question posed to the listener — "I wonder...", "maybe I should...", "is it...?" said mostly to think out loud. See [[8-かも]] for how this differs from かも's probability-hedge role.

## Forming it
Attaches after plain form (verbs, い-adjectives, な-adjective/noun without だ):

- 大丈夫かな。— I wonder if [it]'s okay. / Is it okay, I wonder.
- 明日、晴れるかな。— I wonder if [it]'ll be sunny tomorrow.
- 何時かな。— I wonder what time it is.
- 行こうかな。— Maybe I'll go. (volitional + かな — see [[../2-verb/7-volitional-form]] — softens a decision into a half-formed intention)

## Musing vs. asking — the key distinction
Unlike a real question (〜ですか), かな doesn't strongly demand an answer — it's often said with no listener at all, or said near someone as a soft, low-pressure way of thinking aloud that they *could* respond to but aren't obligated to:

- どうしようかな。— What should [I] do, I wonder. (genuinely undecided, thinking out loud)
- これでいいかな? — Is this okay, I wonder? (softer than これでいいですか — inviting a casual opinion rather than formally asking)

This is why かな shows up constantly in inner monologue, diary writing, and low-key conversational asides — it's the sound of someone actively deciding or checking their own uncertainty in real time.

## かな + negative — hoping/wondering about something not happening
かな pairs with negative form to wonder whether something *won't* happen, often carrying a hopeful undertone:

- 雨、降らないかな。— I hope it doesn't rain. (lit. "I wonder if it won't rain" — reads as a soft wish)
- 誰か来ないかな。— I wonder if someone will come. (hoping someone shows up)

## A note on real-world usage: code-switching into English speech
かな shows up often even when Japanese speakers are otherwise speaking English — especially in mixed-language workplace conversation (e.g. bilingual teams, like at Rakuten). It gets dropped in at the end of an English sentence or as a standalone murmur, because it captures a "musing/half-committed" tone that English doesn't have a single clean equivalent for ("I think so, kana" / "maybe... kana" / just a trailing "...kana~"). Hearing it mixed into English isn't broken grammar — it's a deliberate borrow of the nuance, same function as in pure Japanese: signaling "I'm still deciding/not fully sure," said as much to themselves as to you. Worth recognizing as a genuine, common code-switch rather than an error or quirk specific to one speaker.

## Notes
- かな can double up for extra hesitation: かなあ (drawn out) — even softer, more drawn-out uncertainty.
- Don't confuse か+な (this pattern) with な as the casual masculine command-negation particle (行くな — don't go) — unrelated, context (preceding plain form + full sentence intonation) makes it obvious which is meant.
