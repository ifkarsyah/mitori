# かも(しれない) - "might / maybe"

Hedges a statement as a possibility, not a certainty — "might be," "maybe," "could be." One of the most common ways to soften a claim in daily casual speech.

## Forming it
Attaches after the plain form of verbs/adjectives, or directly after nouns/な-adjective stems (no だ needed) — full form is かもしれない (casual) / かもしれません (polite), often just clipped to かも in speech:

- 行けないかも(しれない)。— [I] might not be able to go.
- 雨が降るかもしれません。— It might rain.
- 明日は休みかも。— [It] might be a day off tomorrow.
- 高いかもしれない。— [It] might be expensive.

## Bare かも — the natural casual clip
In relaxed daily speech, しれない is very often dropped entirely, leaving just かも as a complete, natural sentence-ender:

- A: 明日来る? — Are you coming tomorrow?
- B: かも。— Maybe. (perfectly complete on its own)
- 忘れちゃったかも。— [I] might have forgotten [it]. (see [[4-ちゃう]] for the ちゃう contraction)

## かも vs かな — different kind of uncertainty
Easy to conflate since both hedge, but they point in different directions: かも estimates the *likelihood of a fact* ("it might be true that..."); かな expresses the speaker's own *wondering/musing* ("I wonder..."), often about their own feelings, plans, or something with no clear answer to look up. See [[9-かな]] for the full comparison.

- 雨が降るかも。— It might rain. (assessing an external fact/probability)
- 雨、降るかな。— I wonder if it'll rain. (musing aloud, less committed to an actual probability estimate)

## Notes
- かも is safe in both casual and polite registers (かもしれません for polite, かも/かもしれない for casual) — unlike some fillers, it scales cleanly across formality.
- Stacks naturally with んです/けど for softening explanations: 遅れるかもしれないんですが。— [I] might be late, so...
