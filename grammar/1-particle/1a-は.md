# は - topic particle

Marks what the sentence is *about* — sets the frame, then says something about it. Read as "wa" (not "ha") when used as a particle.

## Core pattern
**X は Y** → "as for X, Y" / "speaking of X, it's Y"

- 私は学生です。— I'm a student. (as for me, [I'm a] student)
- これは本です。— This is a book.
- 東京は大きいです。— Tokyo is big.

## What it actually does
は doesn't mark the grammatical subject — it marks the **topic**. The topic can be the subject, but doesn't have to be:

- 象は鼻が長い。— Elephants, [their] trunk is long. (topic: elephant, subject of 長い: 鼻)
- この店はラーメンがおいしい。— This shop, the ramen is good. (topic: shop, subject: ramen)

This is why は and が can coexist in the same sentence — they're not competing for the same job.

## は vs が (the classic pain point)
Don't try to fully resolve this early — pattern-match instead:

| Use は | Use が |
|---|---|
| Introducing what you're talking about | Introducing new information |
| Known/already-established topic | Answering "who/what" questions |
| General statements, contrast | Specific, in-the-moment observation |
| 私は田中です。(self-intro) | 誉が来た。(が for "who came") |

Quick test: if the question is "what about X?" → は. If the question is "what/who is it?" → が.

- 田中さんは先生です。— Mr. Tanaka is a teacher. (talking about Tanaka)
- だれが先生ですか。— Who is the teacher? / 田中さんが先生です。— Tanaka is [the one who's] the teacher. (が picks him out of a group)

## Contrast use
は can single something out in contrast to something else (often left unsaid):

- 魚は食べますが、肉は食べません。— I eat fish, but I don't eat meat.
- 日本語は話せますが、中国語は話せません。— I can speak Japanese, but not Chinese.

This contrastive は can attach to almost any part of a sentence, not just the subject:

- 今日は行きません。— [Unlike other days,] today I'm not going.

## Negation magnet
Negative sentences pull を/が toward は even when the noun isn't the "topic" in a strong sense — this is extremely common:

- パンを食べません。→ パンは食べません。— I don't eat bread.

## Notes
- は replaces を and が entirely (never は and を/が together on the same noun).
- With に/で/と/から etc., は stacks after the particle: 東京には行きました。(added emphasis/contrast — "to Tokyo, [at least,] I went")
- Topic doesn't have to be repeated once established — Japanese drops it constantly once context is clear.
