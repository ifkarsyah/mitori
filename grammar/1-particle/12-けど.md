# けど - casual "but" particle

Casual version of が (as a clause-connector, not the subject particle covered in [[1b-が]]) and でも — links two clauses in contrast, or just softens a statement. Extremely common in daily speech.

## Basic contrast: "but"
Attaches after plain or polite form, connecting two clauses where the second contradicts or contrasts with the first:

- 行きたいけど、時間がない。— [I] want to go, but [I] don't have time.
- 高いけど、おいしいです。— [It]'s expensive, but [it]'s good.
- 忙しいですけど、大丈夫です。— [I]'m busy, but [it]'s fine. (polite form + けど is fine — けど doesn't force plain form, like から)

Formality ladder: けど (most casual) → けれど → けれども (most formal/written). All three mean the same thing; けど is what you'll hear constantly in daily conversation.

## Softening / cushioning (no real contrast)
Just like the softening use of が in formal speech, けど often appears with nothing actually being contrasted — it just takes the edge off a statement, request, or opinion:

- これ、いいと思うんですけど。— I think this is good, [but/well...]. (hedging an opinion, inviting reaction)
- すみません、ちょっと聞きたいんですけど。— Excuse me, [I] wanted to ask something... (softening a request before making it)

This trailing-off use — ending a sentence right at けど without finishing the thought — is one of the most natural-sounding casual speech habits once you notice it. It leaves room for the listener to respond rather than steamrolling with a complete statement. See [[../8-conjunction/2-trailing-off]] for this pattern across けど/し/から/て-form as a unified habit.

## けど introducing background before the main point
けど is also used to set up context before getting to what you actually want to say, similar to English "so, [background]... anyway, [point]":

- 昨日話したことなんだけど、やっぱり無理かも。— About what [we] talked about yesterday... [I] think it might not work after all.

## Notes
- けど doesn't replace は/が/を — it attaches to the end of a complete clause, not to a noun.
- Don't confuse this けど with けど as a shortened, casual sentence-final softener with no contrast at all — context (is there an actual contrast, or is it just trailing off) tells you which reading applies, both are common.
