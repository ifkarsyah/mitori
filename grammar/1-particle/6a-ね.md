# ね - seeking agreement particle

A sentence-final particle that invites the listener to confirm, agree, or share the feeling — roughly "right?" / "isn't it?" but far more woven into daily speech than that English gloss suggests. See [[6a-よ]] for the contrasting "new info" particle and [[6c-わ]] for the softening particle.

## Seeking agreement / confirmation
The default use — you believe something and are checking the listener agrees or already knows:

- いい天気ですね。— Nice weather, isn't it?
- これ、おいしいですね。— This is tasty, right?
- 明日休みですよね? — Tomorrow's a day off, right? (よね stacks よ's assertion with ね's checking)

## Softening / inviting connection
Even when there's nothing to literally confirm, ね softens a statement and signals "I'm not just broadcasting at you, I want your reaction":

- 頑張りましたね。— [You] worked hard, huh. (empathetic, not really a question)
- そうですね... — Well, let's see... (used constantly as a thinking filler before answering)

そうですね is worth knowing on its own — it's the single most common way to buy a beat before responding, or to agree ("that's right").

## Turn-taking / conversational glue
ね gets dropped in constantly, especially at clause boundaries in casual speech, functioning almost like "you know" — checking the listener is still following:

- 昨日ね、友達とね、映画を見に行ったんです。— So yesterday, right, with a friend, [I] went to see a movie.

This mid-sentence use is very casual/spoken — don't overuse it in writing or formal speech.

## Notes
- ね can attach after plain or polite forms, and after other sentence-final particles (よね, わね).
- Tone matters a lot: rising/soft ね invites genuine agreement; flatter ね is closer to filler. Pattern-matching from listening is more reliable than a rule here.
- Overusing ね is a common learner tell — natives use it, but not on every single sentence.
