# とか - casual "or something/like" particle

Casual, hedging version of と (see [[5a-と]]) for listing examples — signals "this and stuff like it," not an exhaustive list. Extremely common in relaxed daily speech.

## Non-exhaustive listing ("...and stuff", "...or whatever")
Where と lists items completely (see [[5a-と]]), とか implies there are other similar things left unsaid — softer, more casual, "for example":

- パンとかコーヒーとか買いました。— [I] bought bread, coffee, and stuff like that.
- 週末は映画とか見ます。— On weekends [I] watch movies and stuff.

Often only appears once, at the end of a single example, functioning like "or something":

- 疲れたから、休むとか。— [I]'m tired, so maybe [I'll] rest or something. (very casual, trailing suggestion)

## Softening an example or suggestion
とか frequently marks something as just one option among many, keeping the tone light and non-committal — useful for suggestions without sounding pushy:

- お茶とか、どう? — How about tea or something?
- 明日とか、暇? — Are [you] free tomorrow or [some other day nearby]?

## Quoting/hedged reporting (casual version of と + 言う)
とか can casually report what someone said, with a slightly detached, "or something like that" flavor — less precise/committal than と言っていた:

- 田中さん、来ないとか言ってたよ。— Tanaka said something about not coming, [I think].

This carries a "secondhand, not 100% sure of the exact wording" nuance that plain と + 言う doesn't have.

## とか vs や — both non-exhaustive, different register
や (not yet covered as its own file) is the more neutral/written non-exhaustive "and" (パンやコーヒーを買いました。); とか is its casual-speech cousin with the added "or something" hedge. In daily conversation, とか is far more common than や.

## Notes
- とか doesn't replace は/が/を — it attaches directly after the nouns/clauses being listed, and the whole とか-list can still take a particle afterward (パンとかコーヒーとかを買いました. — the final を marks the whole list as the object, though it's often dropped in casual speech per [[2-を]]).
- Overusing とか as a verbal tic (like English "like" or "or whatever") is common in very casual youth speech — recognize it, but don't feel pressured to overuse it yourself.
