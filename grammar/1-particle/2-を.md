# を - direct object particle

Marks the thing a verb acts on. Read as "o" (the を kana is basically only used for this particle).

## Core pattern
**X を Verb** → "[do Verb] to/on X"

- パンを食べます。— I eat bread.
- 本を読みます。— I read a book.
- テレビを見ます。— I watch TV.

## Movement through/away from a space
A distinct use: を marks the space a motion verb passes through or departs from, not something being "acted on":

- 公園を歩きます。— I walk through the park.
- 道を渡ります。— I cross the street.
- 家を出ます。— I leave the house.
- 空を飛ぶ鳥。— A bird that flies through the sky.

Don't confuse this with に/で for destinations/locations — を is specifically the path or point of departure, not a fixed location.

## Dropped constantly in speech
In casual conversation, を is one of the first things to disappear. If the object is clear from context, native speakers routinely skip it — especially with common verbs:

- ご飯食べた? — (Did you) eat? (skipping ご飯を)
- 水ちょうだい。— Give me water. (skipping 水を)
- 何する? — What are you gonna do? (skipping 何を)

This is different from omitting the object entirely (which also happens once it's established in context) — here the noun stays, only the particle drops. It sounds natural and casual, not sloppy; but keep を in polite/written Japanese, and when the sentence would otherwise be ambiguous.

## Notes
- を cannot stack with は/が on the same noun — は or が replaces it under topic/contrast or negation (see [[1a-は]] "negation magnet").
- Some set expressions use を with verbs that don't feel like classic "objects": 旅行をする (to travel), 質問をする (to ask a question) — grammatically the noun+する combo takes を on the noun part.
