# しか - "only" (negative-forced) particle

Also means "only," but structurally different from [[9a-だけ]] in one strict way: しか always requires a negative verb at the end of the sentence, and carries a stronger "that's all there is" undertone — often implying it's less than [you] wanted/expected.

## Must pair with a negative verb
This is the one hard rule to remember — しか + affirmative verb is ungrammatical:

- 千円しかありません。— [I] only have 1000 yen. (lit. "there isn't [anything] except 1000 yen")
- ~~千円しかあります。~~ — ungrammatical, must be negative.
- 日本語しか話せません。— [I] can only speak Japanese [and no other language].
- 少ししか食べなかった。— [I] only ate a little. (implying: less than expected/hoped)

The double-negative logic ("nothing except X") is baked into the grammar even though the English translation reads as a plain positive statement ("I only have...").

## しか vs だけ — the nuance difference
Both can translate to "only," but they don't feel the same:

| | だけ | しか |
|---|---|---|
| Verb form | either | must be negative |
| Tone | neutral | implies insufficiency/regret |
| Example | 水だけ飲みます。(I'll just drink water — neutral choice) | 水しかありません。(There's only water — implying "that's all there is, sorry") |

If you want to express mild disappointment or emphasize scarcity, reach for しか. If you're just stating a plain limit, だけ is safer and more neutral.

## Common daily patterns
- 〜人しかいない — "there are only [N] people" (often implying too few): 三人しかいません。— There are only 3 people [and that's not enough].
- 〜しかない — "there's no choice but to..." (idiomatic, beyond literal "only"): やるしかない。— [There's] nothing to do but try. / [I] have no choice but to do it.

## Notes
- しか replaces は/が/を on the noun it attaches to, same as だけ and も.
- しか can stack after other particles: これしか道がない。— This is the only path [there is].
